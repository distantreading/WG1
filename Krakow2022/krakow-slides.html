Collaborative creation of a multi-lingual literary corpus.

ELTeC is a collection; ELTeC-xxx is a corpus

  • We follow Sinclair (1991) in regarding a corpus as a special kind of collection of texts: 
    “A collection of naturally occurring language text, chosen to characterize a state or variety of a language.” 
     (John Sinclair, Corpus, Concordance, Collocation, 1991)⚓
  • ELTeC corpora are designed, in the sense that their components were chosen using explicit selection criteria
  • ELTeC corpora representative with respect to those criteria, following Biber (1993): 
    “Representativeness refers to the extent to which a sample includes the full range of variability in a population.” 
     (Douglas Biber, Representativeness in Corpus Design, 1993)⚓
  • Hence, the project plan specifies ‘comparable corpora’, each providing a ‘balanced selection of one hundred novels from the period 1840 to 1920’

Easier said than done!

Cross-cultural challenges

  • Defining the population we wish to sample : what is a "novel" ?
  • Defining the sample : what are the selection criteria ?

The answers to these questions turn out to be subtly different in different cultural contexts...

Our selection criteria

  • We specified four ‘balance criteria’ :
    • date of first publication in book form
    • size in pages
    • author gender
    • "canonicity" -- operationalised as the number of times a work was republished
  • Criteria we did not consider include:
    • genre
    • topic
    • intended readership
    • ... and many others
  • Partly on practical grounds but also to avoid the risk of circularity: we wanted to make it possible to classify texts automatically.
  • The goal was to provide good coverage for each criterion, such that the sample would not be dominated by any specific author gender or canonicity-value.
    • This same concern to avoid bias led us to impose an additional constraint that no more than three works should be selected for any given author

What is a novel?

  • Our definition is fairly broad:
    • fictional narrative prose
    • first published between 1840 and 1920
    • first published in the target language
    • of size between 10 and 200 thousand words (so no collected short stories)
    • intended for an adult audience
  • But ...
    • these criteria effectively ruled out some significant language traditions (e.g. nothing or very little of any kind was published in Irish, Latvian, or Lithuanian before 1900)
    • the concepts of “language” and “country” are problematic in the historical European context
    • the ease with which balance criteria could be satisfied varied greatly across language
    • for some criteria, notably author gender, identifying a balanced sample proved impossible.

Language is a problem

The association between languages, countries, and literary traditions is far from being unequivocal or simple.

  • Many European countries (Spain, Switzerland, Belgium, for example) have two or more official languages, each with its own publishing tradition.
  • In many countries, particularly during the 19th century, the language of culture and consequently of publication was not the national language but rather German, French, or English.
  • The same language or a dialectal variant of it may be found in several different countries (Swiss German, or Belgian French, for example).
  • Different languages may be considered the cultural norm in different regions of a single country (Euskera or Catalan in Spain, for example), and will thus have a claim to be represented by their own language corpus.

A pragmatic approach lead us to exclude

  • works by non-European authors (e.g. Henry James)
  • Works published in a language other than that of their original place of publication (e.g. Kafka)

but to include

  • European authors publishing in some language other than their own (e.g. Conrad)
  • additional language corpora such as ELTeC-gsw, ELTeC-eus

Sex is a problem

  • For some language populations, we have extensive data about male and female authorship.
  • For example, the ATCL database lists 22,255 English novels published between 1837 and 1901, produced by a total of 2067 women, 2497 men, and 623 unknown.
  • But this ratio is not consistent over time:

Bassett, Troy J. At the Circulating Library: A Database of Victorian Fiction, 1837–1901. Downloaded from http://www.victorianresearch.org/atcl/visualizations.php.

  • In the ELTeC samples, we know that for some languages, the proportion of female/male authors never rises above 10%

The ELTeC Corpus Composition Criteria Compliance Calculator

  • The E5C is an online tool developed in order to assess objectively the degree of compliance for each corpus.
  • The overall goals of our corpus composition criteria are:
    • to achieve some level of comparability between corpora;
    • to help maximize the variety of novels we make available, particularly novels outside the traditional canon
    • to support a certain number of key methods of text analysis.
  • The E5C defines minimal acceptable and optimal values for each of the following:
    • Encoding level (either 0 or 1; if both are used, a given title can only appear once)
    • Number of texts: 100
    • Author gender: at least 10% female, optimally 30% to 50%
    • Reprint count: at least 30% "low", optimally 40% to 60%
    • Time slot : divided as evenly as possible, i.e. each of the four time slots should contain 20% to 25% of the available titles
    • Size : at least 20% of titles should be short (10-50k) and at least 20% should be long (over 100k).
    • Novels per author: to maximize variation, no author should be represented by more than 3 novels; up to 11 authors may be represented by exactly 3 novels.
  • The E5C gives an overall score for a corpus: values below 50 indicate issues that need to be addressed before the corpus can be included in an ELTeC Release.
  • An extension corpus may be used to hold otherwise eligible novels which cannot be included without distorting the overall corpus balance, or to build up comparable collections outside the temporal scope of ELTeC proper.

State of play

  • There are 17 distinct ELTeC corpora (Czech, German, English, French, Swiss German, Croatian, Hungarian, Italian, Norwegian, Polish, Portuguese, Romanian, Slovenian, Spanish, Serbian, Swedish, and Ukrainian); in total about 107 million words.
  • Only five of these have an E5C score below 75%
  • 5 of them are also marked up to level 2
  • All corpora are available online from gitHub, 12 of them as validated releases from Xenodo
  • Summary and other information at https://distantreading.github.io/ELTeC
  • Coming soon: access via txm, gams, TEI Publisher ...

The future

  • ELTeC as a well-tested benchmark for the development of literary corpora
  • The "extension" concept reapplied to corpora, such as eltec-fra-ext, which use a different date-range
  • And maybe also extending to other literary forms, such as 19th c. drama

Conclusions

  • We learned a great deal about collaboration, about the complexities of cross-cultural comparisons, about finding an appropriate technologies for literary studies ... and about the European novel!
  • We suggest that any effective computational infrastructure for literary studies will need to follow our example, if only in the creation of shared textual resources
